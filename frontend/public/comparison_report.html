1. Overview & Rankings

This report presents an empirical comparison of three independent forecasting approaches applied to TSLA (Tesla Inc.) daily closing prices over a 60-day out-of-sample test window (March-May 2022). Models are evaluated on seven quantitative criteria - RMSE, MAE, MAPE and Directional Accuracy for regression quality, plus Precision, Recall and F1 Score for directional classification quality - each of which is defined in the Performance Results section.

Relative Improvement

The LangGraph Ensemble achieves an RMSE of 28.40 USD, representing a reduction of 28.0% over the ARIMA baseline and 70.8% over the LSTM baseline across the 60-day evaluation window. A lower RMSE indicates that the model's predictions deviate less, on average, from the realised closing price.

Train / Test Split

The test period (March-May 2022) coincides with a significant TSLA price correction - a deliberate choice to assess how each model handles an abrupt regime shift (a structural change in the statistical properties of the data, such as the transition from an uptrend to a downtrend). Models that overfit to the prior bull-market pattern are expected to underperform here.

Key Data Terms

TermDefinition
Univariate series A time series consisting of a single variable measured over time - in this case, the daily closing price. Contrasts with multivariate series which model multiple correlated variables simultaneously.
Walk-forward validation An evaluation strategy where the model predicts one step at a time, then the true observed value is added to the training history before the next prediction. This mirrors real deployment conditions and avoids look-ahead bias (using future data to inform past predictions).
Out-of-sample test window Data held back entirely from model training, used only for final evaluation. Provides an unbiased estimate of how the model will perform on genuinely unseen future data.
Regime shift A structural change in the underlying data-generating process - for example, a sustained trend reversal. Models trained exclusively on one regime (e.g. a bull market) may not generalise to a different regime (e.g. a correction).

3. Model Architectures

Each model represents a distinct class of forecasting approach - classical statistical, deep learning and machine-learning ensemble - enabling a broad methodological comparison on the same dataset and evaluation protocol.

Technical Terms - LangGraph Ensemble

TermDefinition
Moving Average (MA)The arithmetic mean of the closing price over a rolling window of N days (e.g. MA20 = 20-day average). Smooths short-term noise and reveals the underlying trend direction. A price above its MA20 is generally considered in an uptrend.
RSI (Relative Strength Index)A momentum oscillator ranging from 0 to 100 that measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes. Values above 70 indicate overbought conditions; values below 30 suggest oversold. Developed by J. Welles Wilder (1978).
Bollinger %BPositions the current price within the Bollinger Band (a ±2 standard deviation envelope around a 20-day moving average). A value of 1.0 means the price is at the upper band; 0.0 at the lower band. Useful for detecting breakouts and mean-reversion conditions.
MomentumThe percentage change in price over N days, computed as (P_t / P_(t-N)) - 1. Captures the rate of price change; positive momentum indicates an accelerating uptrend, negative momentum a downtrend.
Gradient Boosting (GBR)An ensemble method that builds a sequence of decision trees, each correcting the residual errors of the previous one. Effective at capturing complex non-linear relationships between features and the target variable.
Random Forest (RF)An ensemble of independently trained decision trees whose predictions are averaged. Reduces variance through bagging (bootstrap aggregating), making predictions more stable than a single tree.
Ensemble (weighted)Combining the predictions of multiple models using a fixed weighting scheme. Here, 0.65 × GBR + 0.35 × RF. Ensemble methods generally outperform any single constituent model by reducing both bias and variance.

Technical Terms - ARIMA

TermDefinition
ARIMA(p, d, q)AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average. A classical time-series model combining three components: AR (autoregressive - the influence of p past values), I (integrated - d rounds of differencing to achieve stationarity) and MA (moving average of q past forecast errors).
Autoregressive (AR) order pThe number of lagged price values used as predictors. AR(2) means the current price is modelled as a linear combination of the two most recent prices plus a noise term: X_t = c + φ₁X_(t-1) + φ₂X_(t-2) + ε_t.
Differencing order dThe number of times the series is differenced (subtracted from its own lag) to remove trends and achieve stationarity. A stationary series has constant mean and variance over time, which ARIMA requires. d=0 means no differencing was needed.
StationarityA time series is stationary when its statistical properties (mean, variance, autocorrelation) do not change over time. Required by ARIMA. Verified using the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test, where a p-value below 0.05 rejects the null hypothesis of non-stationarity.
ADF TestAugmented Dickey-Fuller test - a statistical hypothesis test for the presence of a unit root (a source of non-stationarity) in a time series. A low p-value indicates the series is stationary.
Grid searchExhaustive evaluation of all combinations of hyperparameter values within a specified range. Here, all 27 combinations of (p,d,q) ∈ [0,2]³ were evaluated by walk-forward RMSE and the combination yielding the lowest error was selected.

Technical Terms - LSTM

TermDefinition
LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)A type of Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) designed to learn long-range temporal dependencies. Each cell contains three gates - input, forget and output - which control what information is retained, discarded, or passed forward. This gated structure solves the vanishing gradient problem that limits standard RNNs.
Hidden unitsThe dimensionality of the LSTM's internal state vector. More units allow the network to represent more complex patterns, but also increase the risk of overfitting on small datasets. Layer 1 uses 64 units; Layer 2 uses 32.
Lookback window (sequence length)The number of consecutive historical time steps fed as a single input to the LSTM. A window of 60 means the model sees the past 60 trading days (approximately three calendar months) before making a prediction.
DropoutA regularisation technique where a random fraction of neurons (here 20%) are temporarily deactivated during each training step. This prevents co-adaptation of neurons and reduces overfitting by forcing the network to learn redundant representations.
MinMax normalisationScales all values to the range [0, 1] using x_norm = (x - x_min) / (x_max - x_min). Required for LSTM because the network is sensitive to the scale of inputs; unscaled prices can cause unstable gradients during training.
EpochOne complete pass through the entire training dataset. The model's weights are updated after each batch within an epoch. Training for 100 epochs means the model sees all training sequences 100 times.
Adam optimiserAn adaptive learning-rate optimisation algorithm (Adaptive Moment Estimation) that maintains per-parameter learning rates based on first and second moment estimates of the gradients. Generally converges faster than standard stochastic gradient descent.
MSE lossMean Squared Error - the average squared difference between predicted and actual (normalised) values, used as the training objective. Minimising MSE during training is equivalent to minimising RMSE on the training set.
OverfittingWhen a model learns the specific patterns and noise of the training data so precisely that it loses the ability to generalise to new data. Signs include very low training error but high test error. Exacerbated here by a small dataset (698 sequences) and 100 training epochs without early stopping.
VADER (Sentiment)Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner - a lexicon-based sentiment analysis tool optimised for short financial texts. Used in the original paper (but not this implementation) to generate sentiment scores from news headlines, which are fed alongside price data into the LSTM.
The paper attributes an 8-12% MAPE improvement specifically to sentiment enrichment.

4. Performance Results

Metric Definitions 7 evaluation metrics explained

All seven metrics below are computed on the 60-day out-of-sample test window using walk-forward predictions. Regression metrics (RMSE/MAE/MAPE) measure price-level accuracy; classification metrics (Precision/Recall/F1) treat directional prediction as a binary classification task (up=1, down=0).

MetricFormulaUnitWhat it measuresSensitivity
RMSE
Root Mean Squared Error		 sqrt( mean( (y - ŷ)² ) ) USD The square root of the average squared difference between actual and predicted prices. Expressed in the same unit as the price, making it directly interpretable. Sensitive to large errors - a single large deviation inflates RMSE disproportionately due to squaring.
MAE
Mean Absolute Error		 mean( |y - ŷ| ) USD The average magnitude of prediction errors without squaring. Treats all deviations equally regardless of their size. More robust to outlier predictions than RMSE. A model with lower MAE makes fewer consistently large errors.
MAPE
Mean Absolute Percentage Error		 mean( |y - ŷ| / y ) × 100 % The average prediction error expressed as a percentage of the actual price. Scale-independent, making it comparable across different stocks or price levels. Can be unstable when actual prices approach zero; reliable for TSLA's price range ($35-$1,230).
Directional Accuracy mean( sign(Δy) == sign(Δŷ) ) × 100 % The percentage of test days where the model correctly predicted the direction of price movement (up vs. down) relative to the previous day. A random predictor achieves approximately 50%. Directly relevant to trading signal quality. A model with high directional accuracy is more useful for buy/sell decision-making, even if its absolute price errors are moderate.
Precision
Up-day Precision		 TP / (TP + FP) % Of all days the model predicted price would rise, the fraction where the price actually rose. Answers: "When the model says 'buy', how often is it right?" Low precision = many false buy signals (predicting up when price actually falls). Penalises aggressive upward-bias models.
Recall
Up-day Sensitivity		 TP / (TP + FN) % Of all days where the price actually rose, the fraction correctly identified as up-moves. Answers: "How many actual up-days did the model catch?" Low recall = model misses most upward moves (predicts down when price actually rises). Penalises overly conservative models.
F1 Score
Harmonic mean P·R		 2 · P · R / (P + R) 0-1 Combines Precision and Recall into a single score. A model must achieve both - high F1 means it finds most real up-moves without generating excessive false signals. Best single metric for imbalanced or practically-relevant directional evaluation. Penalises models that trade off one dimension to inflate the other.

6. Error Analysis

Beyond aggregate metrics, examining the distribution and pattern of prediction errors reveals structural characteristics of each model - such as systematic bias (consistent over- or under-estimation) and the handling of directional market signals.

Residual Distributions

Residual: The difference between the actual price and the model's predicted price at each time step - i.e., residual = actual - predicted. A residual of zero means a perfect prediction. Positive residuals indicate the model underestimated the price; negative residuals indicate overestimation. An ideal residual distribution is centred near zero (no systematic bias) with low spread (small typical errors) and an approximately normal shape.

Directional Accuracy

Directional Accuracy measures whether the model correctly predicted the sign of the price change on each test day - that is, whether the price would be higher or lower than the previous day's close. A value of 50% corresponds to random chance (equivalent to a coin flip). Values above 50% indicate genuine directional predictive power, which is directly actionable for trading strategies.

Classification Metrics & Confusion Matrices

Directional prediction is treated as a binary classification problem: positive class = price rises (next close > previous close), negative class = price falls. Over the 60-day test window there are 59 directional comparisons (via first difference), with 29 actual up-days and 30 actual down-days.

Reading a confusion matrix: TP = correctly predicted up  |  TN = correctly predicted down  |  FP = predicted up but price fell (false buy signal)  |  FN = predicted down but price rose (missed up-move)

LSTM directional bias: The LSTM predicts "down" on 26 of 59 days (TN=23, FP=7 → only 30 predicted up) vs. 29 actual up-days. Its downward-biased predictions during the May 2022 correction give higher precision (58.8%) but very low recall (34.5%) - it correctly avoids false buy signals but misses most genuine price rises. The F1 penalty (0.435 vs 0.882) quantifies this imbalance clearly, something Directional Accuracy alone (55.9%) understates.

Key Insights

FindingDetail
Rich features beat raw prices LangGraph's 20 engineered features (RSI, MAs, momentum, Bollinger) give it superior signal quality vs ARIMA's 2-lag autoregression.
Ensemble diversification Combining GBR (captures non-linear interactions) and RF (variance reduction) reduces prediction variance on the volatile test period.
ARIMA adapts locally Walk-forward refitting lets ARIMA track the downtrend incrementally, outperforming LSTM which was fitted once and frozen.
LSTM regime generalisation Without sentiment augmentation, the LSTM trained on a bull market struggles to generalise to the sharp May 2022 correction. The paper's reported 2.72% MAPE includes VADER sentiment - absent here.
F1 reveals LSTM's hidden weakness LSTM's Directional Accuracy (55.9%) looks mediocre but masks a more serious problem: its Recall is only 34.5%, meaning it misses 65% of genuine up-moves. F1=0.435 vs LangGraph's F1=0.882 quantifies this gap more clearly than accuracy alone - a critical distinction for buy-side signal generation.
ARIMA is directionally uninformative Precision=50.0%, Recall=51.7%, F1=0.508 - statistically indistinguishable from a random coin flip. ARIMA's competitive RMSE stems from it tracking the overall downtrend in price level, not from genuine directional skill. For trading-signal use-cases, it offers no edge.
LangGraph architecture advantage The 4-node pipeline (feature engineering → signal generation → ensemble → calibration) mirrors the Diversifi backend's modular node-based design, making it maintainable, extensible and interpretable.

Improvement Opportunities

  • LangGraph: Add VADER sentiment from news headlines (like the paper) - expected 5-10% further MAPE reduction.
  • ARIMA: Upgrade to SARIMAX with exogenous volume and macro indicators.
  • LSTM: Integrate VADER sentiment (paper's key contribution); use attention mechanism; retrain on more diverse price regimes.
  • All models: Ensemble all three together for meta-prediction to combine their complementary strengths.
