2. Technical Indicators

Technical indicators are calculated purely from a stock's price and volume history — no balance sheets involved. They help identify trends, momentum shifts, and potential reversal points.

RSI — Relative Strength Index

What it is: A 0–100 oscillator that measures how fast and how much a price has moved recently. Calculated over 14 trading days by default.

RSI = 100 − ( 100 ÷ (1 + RS) )
RS = Average Gain over 14 days ÷ Average Loss over 14 days
RSI valueInterpretation
> 70Overbought — stock may be due for a pullback
50 – 70Bullish zone — upward momentum
30 – 50Neutral to slightly bearish
< 30Oversold — potential bounce opportunity
DiversiFi uses RSI ≥ 60 as the high-score threshold (15 pts). A reading of 60 doesn't mean overbought — it signals healthy, sustained buying interest.

MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence

What it is: The difference between a 12-day and 26-day Exponential Moving Average (EMA). A 9-day EMA of that difference is called the "signal line."

MACD Line = EMA(12) − EMA(26)
Signal Line = EMA(9) of MACD Line
Histogram = MACD Line − Signal Line
  • Bullish crossover: MACD line crosses above signal line → buying pressure increasing
  • Bearish crossover: MACD line drops below signal line → momentum fading

In DiversiFi: MACD bullish = +8 pts, bearish = +2 pts in the Signal Score.

Moving Averages — SMA vs EMA

SMA (Simple Moving Average): The plain average of closing prices over N days. Each day has equal weight.

SMA(N) = (P₁ + P₂ + … + Pₙ) ÷ N

EMA (Exponential Moving Average): Weights recent prices more heavily. Reacts faster to new data than SMA.

EMA = Price × k + EMA(prev) × (1 − k)    where k = 2 ÷ (N + 1)

20DMA and 50DMA

The 20-day and 50-day Simple Moving Averages are the two most-watched trend filters in Indian equities trading.

ConfigurationSignal ScoreMeaning
Price > 20DMA > 50DMA12 ptsFull bullish stack — both short and medium-term trends up
Price > 20DMA only8 ptsShort-term bullish, medium-term mixed
Price > 50DMA only4 ptsMedium-term support holding but short-term weak
Price < both DMAs1 ptDowntrend — both moving averages acting as resistance
A "golden cross" (20DMA crossing above 50DMA) is one of the strongest bullish signals in technical analysis. A "death cross" (20DMA falling below 50DMA) is bearish.

Bollinger Bands

What they are: A 20-day SMA surrounded by bands set ±2 standard deviations apart. They automatically widen during volatile periods and narrow during quiet ones.

Middle Band = SMA(20)
Upper Band = SMA(20) + 2 × StdDev(20)
Lower Band = SMA(20) − 2 × StdDev(20)
  • Price touching the upper band: stock is relatively expensive vs recent range — watch for reversal
  • Price touching the lower band: oversold relative to recent range — watch for bounce
  • Band squeeze (bands tightening): low volatility phase, often precedes a big move

Volume

Volume is the number of shares traded in a session. DiversiFi calculates a volume surge ratio: the 5-day average divided by the 30-day average.

Volume Surge = (avg volume last 5 days) ÷ (avg volume last 30 days)

A ratio > 1.5 means today's trading is 50% heavier than normal — which confirms a price move is driven by real conviction, not just noise.

3. Portfolio Metrics — Beta, Alpha, Sharpe

These three metrics tell you whether your portfolio is taking the right amount of risk and whether that risk is being rewarded. DiversiFi computes all three using 6 months of daily prices benchmarked against Nifty 50 (^NSEI) or Nifty 500 (^CRSLDX).

Beta — Market Sensitivity

What it means: How much your portfolio moves when the overall market moves. A beta of 1.0 means your portfolio tracks the market exactly.

Beta = Covariance(portfolio daily returns, benchmark daily returns)
÷ Variance(benchmark daily returns)
Beta valueWhat it means
< 0.8Defensive — less volatile than market. Good for conservative investors.
0.8 – 1.2Market-neutral — moves roughly with the index.
> 1.2Aggressive — amplifies market moves. Higher risk, higher potential reward.
NegativeMoves opposite to market (rare — e.g. gold miners during crash)
DiversiFi algo paper-trading portfolio had a beta of 0.74 — meaning it captured market upside with less downside exposure.

Alpha — Excess Return

What it means: How much return you earned above and beyond what you were compensated for by simply taking market risk. Positive alpha = skill (or luck). Negative alpha = underperformance.

Alpha = Portfolio Annual Return
− [ Risk-Free Rate + Beta × (Benchmark Return − Risk-Free Rate) ]

This is the CAPM formula (Capital Asset Pricing Model). The risk-free rate used is the approximate Indian T-bill rate (≈ 6.5%). The term in brackets is what you should have earned given your beta — alpha is what you earned on top of that.

If your portfolio returned 18% and CAPM says you should have earned 14% given your beta, your alpha is +4%. That's genuine outperformance.

Sharpe Ratio — Risk-Adjusted Return

What it means: How much return you got for each unit of volatility you accepted. Higher is better. A Sharpe of 1.0+ is considered good; 2.0+ is excellent.

Sharpe = (Portfolio Annual Return − Risk-Free Rate)
÷ (Portfolio Daily StdDev × √252)

The × √252 converts daily volatility to annual volatility (252 trading days in a year).

Sharpe RatioAssessment
< 0Losing money on a risk-adjusted basis
0 – 0.5Poor — not being compensated for the risk taken
0.5 – 1.0Acceptable
1.0 – 2.0Good
> 2.0Excellent (rare, often unsustainable)
DiversiFi's algo paper-trading portfolio achieved a Sharpe of 1.84 over H1 2025, indicating strong risk-adjusted returns against Nifty 50.

6-Month Return vs Benchmark

A simple comparison: your portfolio's cumulative return over 6 months vs the benchmark (Nifty 50 or Nifty 500). Expressed as a percentage. If the market went up 8% and your portfolio went up 12%, you outperformed by 4 percentage points.

P&L (Profit and Loss)

The total gain or loss on your holdings. DiversiFi shows:

  • Absolute P&L (₹): Current Value − Amount Invested
  • P&L %: (Absolute P&L ÷ Invested) × 100

Consistency Score (max 25 pts)

Consistency measures how steadily the fund has performed — avoiding extreme drawdowns and delivering year-on-year growth. A fund that returned 15% every year scores higher than one that returned 60% one year and lost 30% the next, even if average returns are similar.

Consistency is calculated from the fund's NAV history: low volatility of annual returns + absence of large negative years = high consistency score.

5. Mutual Fund Terms

NAV — Net Asset Value

What it is: The price of one unit of a mutual fund. Calculated daily after market close.

NAV = (Total Assets of Fund − Liabilities) ÷ Number of Units Outstanding

If a fund's NAV is ₹45 and you invest ₹10,000, you receive 10,000 ÷ 45 = 222.22 units. When the fund's holdings rise in value, the NAV rises too, and your investment grows.

A high NAV (e.g. ₹500) vs a low NAV (e.g. ₹15) does NOT mean one fund is better or more expensive than the other. What matters is percentage returns, not the absolute NAV price.

AUM — Assets Under Management

The total market value of all money invested in a fund. Large AUM (> ₹10,000 crore) means the fund is popular and liquid, but very large funds in small/mid-cap categories can struggle to deploy capital efficiently.

Expense Ratio

The annual fee the fund house charges for managing the fund, expressed as a percentage of AUM. This is deducted daily from NAV — you never pay it directly.

Fund typeTypical expense ratio
Index Fund / ETF0.1% – 0.3%
Large Cap active fund0.5% – 1.5%
Mid / Small Cap active1.0% – 2.0%
ELSS (tax-saving)1.0% – 2.5%
Over 20 years, a 1.5% vs 0.3% expense ratio difference can cost you 20–25% of final corpus due to compounding drag. Always compare expense ratios.

SIP — Systematic Investment Plan

A method of investing a fixed amount at regular intervals (monthly, weekly) regardless of market conditions. The key benefit is Rupee Cost Averaging: you automatically buy more units when prices are low and fewer when prices are high.

SIP Corpus Formula

DiversiFi projects the future value of a SIP using the standard future value of an annuity formula:

Corpus = P × [ ((1 + r)ⁿ − 1) ÷ r ] × (1 + r)

Where:
P = Monthly SIP amount (₹)
r = Monthly interest rate = Annual Rate ÷ 12
n = Total months = Years × 12

Example: ₹10,000/month for 10 years at 12% p.a.

r = 12% ÷ 12 = 1% = 0.01
n = 10 × 12 = 120 months
Corpus = 10000 × [ (1.01¹²⁰ − 1) / 0.01 ] × 1.01
       ≈ ₹23.2 lakhs (invested ₹12 lakhs, gain ₹11.2 lakhs)

Lumpsum Investment

A one-time investment instead of periodic contributions. Suitable when markets have corrected sharply or when you receive a windfall. Higher risk than SIP because you are fully exposed to market timing.

Sector Spread (max 25 pts)

Counts how many of the 8 major NSE sectors you have meaningful exposure to. More active sectors = better spread.

Sector Spread pts = min(25, round((activeSectors ÷ 8) × 25))

Asset Class Spread (max 20 pts)

How many distinct asset types you hold: stocks, large-cap MFs, mid-cap MFs, small-cap MFs, debt/hybrid funds, ELSS, etc. Each distinct type adds 7 pts up to 20.

Concentration Risk

Flags when any single position dominates the portfolio. DiversiFi raises a concentration flag when:

  • Any single stock exceeds 15% of the stock portion of your portfolio
  • Any single sector exceeds 25% of the total portfolio
  • Top 3 holdings together exceed 50% of portfolio
Concentration risk is evaluated on stock value only (not including MF value) for stock-level checks — including MF NAV would unfairly dilute the signal and hide genuine over-concentration in individual stocks.

Sector Allocation vs Benchmark

Your sector weights are compared to the Nifty 50 or Nifty 500 benchmark weights. A delta > +5% = overweight, < −5% = underweight. Having ≥ 5 sectors within ±10% of benchmark adds 10 pts to the diversification score.

7. Fundamental Metrics

Fundamental metrics come from a company's financial statements — not from price charts. They tell you whether a business is healthy, growing, and reasonably valued.

PE Ratio — Price to Earnings

What it is: How much the market is willing to pay for ₹1 of the company's earnings.

PE = Market Price per Share ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)
PE RangeInterpretation (Indian market context)Signal Score
< 0Company is loss-making3 pts
≤ 20Cheap / value zone12 pts
≤ 35Fair value for quality companies9 pts
≤ 50Expensive but justifiable for high-growth6 pts
> 50Very expensive — requires exceptional growth4 pts
Nifty 50 PE historically trades between 18–28. PE > 30 is expensive by historical standards. PE of growth stocks (like tech) can legitimately be 50–100 if earnings are growing fast.

PB Ratio — Price to Book

What it is: The ratio of market price to the company's book value (net assets per share). Useful for asset-heavy industries like banks, NBFCs, and industrials.

PB = Market Price ÷ (Total Assets − Total Liabilities) per share
  • PB < 1: Trading below net assets — potentially undervalued (or the assets are impaired)
  • PB 1–3: Fair value range for most Indian companies
  • PB > 5: Premium valuation — market expects high returns on equity

EPS — Earnings Per Share

The share of annual net profit attributable to each outstanding share.

EPS = Net Profit After Tax ÷ Total Shares Outstanding

Positive EPS means the company is profitable. DiversiFi awards +8 pts in the Signal Score for positive EPS, and +3 pts for loss-making companies.

ROE — Return on Equity

What it is: How much profit the company generates for each rupee of shareholders' capital. A measure of management efficiency.

ROE = Net Profit ÷ Shareholder Equity × 100%

ROE > 15% is generally considered good for Indian businesses. High ROE + reasonable debt = quality business.

D/E Ratio — Debt to Equity

How much debt the company carries relative to its equity base.

D/E = Total Debt ÷ Total Shareholders' Equity
  • D/E < 0.5: Conservative, low financial risk
  • D/E 0.5–1.5: Moderate leverage, normal for most industries
  • D/E > 2: High leverage — interest payments may eat into profits in downturns
  • Capital-intensive industries (power, infra, banking) naturally carry higher D/E

Win Rate

The percentage of trades that closed with a profit.

Win Rate = (Profitable Trades ÷ Total Trades) × 100%

A 64.2% win rate on 47 trades means 30 profitable trades out of 47. Professional systematic strategies typically target 55–65%. Higher win rates alone are not sufficient — the magnitude of wins vs losses also matters (Risk:Reward ratio).

Max Drawdown

The largest peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value during the period. Measures worst-case loss if you had invested at the peak and exited at the trough.

Max Drawdown = (Trough Value − Peak Value) ÷ Peak Value × 100%

DiversiFi algo recorded −8.3% max drawdown. For context, Nifty 50 has had drawdowns of −50% (2008), −40% (2020). A −8.3% max drawdown is very conservative.

VaR — Value at Risk

A statistical measure of the maximum loss you should expect on a single day, with 95% confidence. DiversiFi's algo showed VaR (95%, 1d) = −1.2%.

VaR (95%, 1d) = −1.2% means: on 95% of trading days, your daily loss should not exceed 1.2%. On the remaining 5% of days (the tail), losses could exceed this. VaR does NOT tell you how bad those tail losses could be — that requires CVaR (Conditional VaR).

Annualised Alpha

Alpha calculated and expressed as a yearly figure, making it comparable across strategies of different durations.

Annualised Alpha = CAPM Alpha × (252 ÷ trading days in period)

DiversiFi algo: +14.4% annualised alpha over Nifty 50, meaning the strategy generated 14.4 percentage points of excess return per year above what its beta predicted.

9. Market Structure — FII, DII, Indices, Sectors

FII — Foreign Institutional Investors

Large foreign entities (pension funds, hedge funds, sovereign wealth funds) that invest in Indian markets. FII flows are a major driver of Nifty 50 direction.

MetricMeaning
FII BuyTotal value of Indian stocks/bonds purchased by FIIs today (₹ crore)
FII SellTotal value sold
FII NetBuy − Sell. Positive = net inflow into India. Negative = capital leaving India.
Large FII outflows (net selling) often coincide with rupee depreciation and Nifty corrections. A sustained 5+ session FII sell streak is a red flag for portfolio risk.

DII — Domestic Institutional Investors

Indian institutions — mutual funds, insurance companies (LIC), pension funds. DIIs often buy when FIIs sell, providing a stabilising counter-force to foreign volatility.

When FII Net is deeply negative but DII Net is strongly positive, the market often holds support — domestic money absorbs the foreign selling. This is why SEBI's push for SIP culture (₹20,000+ crore/month SIP inflows) has made Indian markets more resilient.

Forex — USD/INR Impact

  • Weak rupee (USD/INR rising): Good for IT exporters (earn USD, report in INR — revenue goes up in rupee terms). Bad for oil importers (crude is priced in USD — import bill rises).
  • RBI intervention threshold: When USD/INR approaches ₹84, the Reserve Bank of India typically intervenes by selling USD reserves to support the rupee.

Yen Carry Trade

Investors borrow cheaply in Japanese Yen (historically near 0% interest) and invest the proceeds in higher-yielding assets like Indian equities. When the Bank of Japan raises rates or JPY appreciates sharply, these trades unwind — investors sell Indian (and other EM) assets to repay yen loans, causing sudden FII outflows and market drops.

The August 2024 global correction was partly triggered by a yen carry trade unwind after the Bank of Japan raised rates unexpectedly — Nifty fell ~4% in a single week.

11. Monte Carlo Simulation & Scenario Analysis

What is Monte Carlo Simulation?

A technique that runs thousands of randomised "what-if" scenarios for your portfolio's future value. Instead of giving you one projected outcome, it gives you a probability distribution — showing the range of possible outcomes and how likely each is.

DiversiFi runs 1,000+ simulations per portfolio using Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM) — the same mathematical model used by professional options traders and risk managers.

Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM)

The standard model for stock price paths. It assumes prices grow exponentially (multiplicative, not additive) and that the random day-to-day fluctuations follow a normal distribution.

dS = μS·dt + σS·dW

Where:
S = asset price
μ = expected drift (annualised return − variance adjustment)
σ = volatility (annualised standard deviation of returns)
dW = random Wiener process increment (normally distributed noise)

In practice, each simulation uses randomNormal(mean, stdDev) to generate daily return shocks, compounded month by month.

CAGR — Compound Annual Growth Rate

The steady-state annual growth rate that would turn your initial investment into the final value over the investment period.

CAGR = (Final Value ÷ Initial Value)^(1 ÷ years) − 1

Example: ₹1 lakh grows to ₹2.5 lakhs in 8 years → CAGR = (2.5)^(1/8) − 1 = 12.1% p.a.

Percentile Outcomes

After running 1,000 simulations, DiversiFi shows you:

PercentileMeaning
10th percentilePessimistic outcome — only 10% of scenarios do worse than this
50th percentile (median)Middle outcome — half of scenarios are above, half below
90th percentileOptimistic outcome — only 10% of scenarios do better than this

Industry & Geopolitical Scenarios

On top of random GBM noise, DiversiFi overlays structured macro scenarios:

  • Industry scenarios: E.g. "IT sector slowdown" — applies a −20% impact shock to IT holdings starting in a specified month
  • Geopolitical scenarios: E.g. "Global recession" — applies a broad negative impact across all holdings, with degree proportional to each company's global revenue exposure
  • AI holding impact analysis: For each holding, an LLM evaluates whether it would be affected by each scenario and estimates the impact percentage with reasoning
Simulation results are illustrative estimates, not guarantees. Real markets can exhibit fat tails, correlation breakdowns, and liquidity crises not captured by GBM.
